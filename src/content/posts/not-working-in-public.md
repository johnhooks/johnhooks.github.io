For the last three years, most of my engineering attention went into building an application with a team. I read Laravel source code, debugged production issues, and became a better engineer in ordinary, unglamorous ways.

That work matters to me. But it lives inside a company. Tickets, pull requests, Slack threads, private decisions, all the context that makes a technical choice make sense. Real work, but nothing in public.

I do not think the answer is to “work in public,” at least not in the way that phrase usually lands.

I do not want to narrate every commit. I do not want a productivity feed where the work has to be staged as evidence that I am doing the work.

I also do not like social media very much. Facebook and Twitter have always felt toxic to me in different ways. I still check Twitter once in a while, but I rarely come away feeling more engaged with the work, or with people, or with myself.

It is not a place I want to build around.

What I want is simpler and stranger than that.

<line-emphasis>I want a home on the web.</line-emphasis>

A place to operate from. A place where projects can have pages and notes can turn into posts. A place where small experiments can exist without needing to become companies or campaigns or proof of anything. A place where I can put a weird little product next to a half-formed thought next to a bug I finally understood.

Not because every piece deserves an audience. Because the web is better when people leave specific things lying around in public.

Some of that will be writing. Some of it will be project journals. Some of it will be small tools. Some of it will probably be silly. These are not portfolio pieces in the usual sense. They are places where my attention is alive.

There is a practical reason too. I do not want the next few years of learning to disappear into private systems by default. Not everything needs to be public. Not the confidential parts. Just the parts that are mine to carry forward.

So the intent is not really to work in public.

It is to work from somewhere.

To leave tracks. To make my corner of the web a little stranger and more useful. To have a place to come back to when a project gets weird enough that it needs a room of its own.
